The Dataset

  • Who collected/published the data?
    The Seattle Public Library collected/published the data.

  • What are the parameters of the data (dates, number of checkouts, kinds of books, etc.)? UsageClass(item is physical or digital), CheckoutType(the vendor tool used to check out the item), MaterialType(the type of item checked out), CheckoutYear(the year of checkout), CheckoutMonth(the month of checkout), Checkouts(the number of checkouts), Title(the title of the checkout book), ISBN, Creator, Subjects, Publisher, PublicationYear.

  • How was the data collected or generated?
    Checkout information is gathered from a variety of recent and old sources. The media suppliers Overdrive, hoopla, Freegal, and RBDigital give usage information for digital items. The Legrady artwork data archives are the source of this information for historical physical item checkouts between April 2005 and September 30, 2016. The Horizon ILS has been as the source for data since October 1, 2016.

  • Why was the data collected?
    In libraries, information on checkouts is gathered to monitor book popularity and circulation, to examine reading behavior trends, to design programs and events, and to assess the efficiency of library services.

  • What, if any, ethical questions do you need to consider when working with this data?
    The information on individual book checkouts might be used to identify people or divulge personal information, raising privacy issues.

  • What are possible limitations or problems with this data? (at least 200 words)

First of all, the data might be skewed toward certain book genres or readers, such as those who borrow just books in a particular category or go to the library more frequently. It is helpful for people to analyze the data if the library can collect the specific time when does people checkout. Based on this data, we can conclude when does people prefer to go to the library to pick books they want. Furthermore, the information in the data is completely devoid of context and details on the motives behind the checkouts, such as whether they were for pleasure or academic pursuits. Seasonality, holidays, and other special occasions, which can modify reader behavior and reading habits, may have an impact on the results. As it was mentioned by the Seattle Public Library, the data is renewed every month. In my opinion, it is necessary to collect the data more frequently, like once a week or once in two weeks, which will obtain the data more precisely. Last but not the least, books that were borrowed but never returned as well as lost or damaged books that had to be replaced may not be taken into account in the data. It may be difficult to draw conclusions about the librarys overall circulation trends because the data only represent a small portion of the collection as a whole.
